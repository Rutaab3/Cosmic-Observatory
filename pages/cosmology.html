Cosmology

The study of the universe's origin, evolution, and ultimate fate

What is Cosmology?

Cosmology is the scientific study of the universe as a whole, including its origin, evolution, structure, and ultimate fate. It combines observations, theoretical physics, and mathematical models to understand the largest scales of space and time.

Modern cosmology is built on Einstein's theory of general relativity and the cosmological principle, which assumes the universe is homogeneous and isotropic on large scales. This field has revolutionized our understanding of space, time, and matter.

Age: 13.8 billion years Observable diameter: 93 billion ly Expanding universe Dark matter & energy

The Big Bang Theory

The prevailing model for the universe's origin and early evolution

Timeline of the Universe

Planck Epoch (0 to 10⁻⁴³ seconds)

The earliest moment we can theoretically describe, when all fundamental forces were unified. Physics as we know it breaks down at this scale.

Quantum gravity era Unified forces Planck temperature
Inflation (10⁻³⁶ to 10⁻³² seconds)

A period of exponential expansion that explains the universe's large-scale uniformity and flatness. The universe expanded by a factor of at least 10²⁶.

Exponential expansion Horizon problem solved Quantum fluctuations
Nucleosynthesis (3-20 minutes)

The universe cooled enough for protons and neutrons to form light elements: hydrogen, helium, and trace amounts of lithium and beryllium.

75% hydrogen 25% helium Temperature: 10⁹ K
Recombination (380,000 years)

Electrons combined with nuclei to form neutral atoms, making the universe transparent to light. This is when the cosmic microwave background was emitted.

First neutral atoms Universe becomes transparent CMB emission
Dark Ages (380,000 - 150 million years)

A period with no stars or galaxies, only neutral hydrogen and helium gas slowly clumping under gravity's influence.

No light sources Structure formation begins Dark matter halos
Reionization (150 million - 1 billion years)

The first stars and galaxies formed, their radiation ionizing the surrounding hydrogen and ending the cosmic dark ages.

First stars Galaxy formation Universe reionized

Dark Universe

The mysterious components that dominate the cosmos

Dark Matter

Dark matter makes up about 27% of the universe but doesn't emit, absorb, or reflect light. We know it exists because of its gravitational effects on visible matter and the cosmic microwave background.

Evidence for Dark Matter:

Galaxy Rotation Curves: Stars in galaxy outskirts move too fast for visible matter alone.

Gravitational Lensing: Light bends around invisible mass concentrations.

Cosmic Microwave Background: Patterns require dark matter for structure formation.

Candidates:
WIMPs Axions Sterile neutrinos Primordial black holes

Dark Energy

Dark energy comprises about 68% of the universe and is responsible for its accelerating expansion. Discovered in 1998, it remains one of the biggest mysteries in physics.

Discovery:

Observations of distant supernovae showed the universe's expansion is accelerating, contrary to expectations that gravity should slow it down.

Possible Explanations:

Cosmological Constant: Einstein's lambda, representing vacuum energy.

Quintessence: A dynamic scalar field that changes over time.

Modified Gravity: Alterations to general relativity on cosmic scales.

68% of universe Accelerating expansion Negative pressure

Cosmic Microwave Background

The afterglow of the Big Bang and our window to the early universe

The Universe's Baby Picture

The cosmic microwave background (CMB) is radiation left over from the Big Bang, emitted when the universe first became transparent about 380,000 years after the Big Bang. It provides crucial evidence for the Big Bang theory and insights into the universe's composition and geometry.

Discovery and Significance:

Discovered accidentally by Penzias and Wilson in 1965, the CMB earned them the Nobel Prize. It appears as a nearly uniform glow across the entire sky with tiny temperature fluctuations that seeded all cosmic structure.

Key Properties:

Temperature: 2.725 K, nearly uniform across the sky

Blackbody Spectrum: Perfect thermal radiation, confirming Big Bang predictions

Anisotropies: Tiny fluctuations (10⁻⁵ K) that grew into galaxies and clusters

What CMB Tells Us:

The CMB's precise measurements have determined fundamental cosmological parameters: the universe's age (13.8 billion years), composition (5% ordinary matter, 27% dark matter, 68% dark energy), and geometry (flat).

CMB Missions

COBE (1989-1993)

First detailed CMB measurements, discovered anisotropies, Nobel Prize 2006.

WMAP (2001-2010)

Precise measurements of CMB temperature and polarization patterns.

Planck (2009-2013)

Most detailed CMB map ever, refined cosmological parameters to high precision.

The Universe's Fate

Possible scenarios for the ultimate destiny of the cosmos

Heat Death

The most likely scenario based on current observations. The universe will continue expanding and cooling until it reaches maximum entropy, with all energy evenly distributed and no more work possible.

Timeline:

10¹⁴ years: Star formation ends as gas is exhausted

10¹⁵ years: Stars begin dying, leaving white dwarfs, neutron stars, and black holes

10³⁴ years: Protons decay (if they do), leaving only leptons and photons

10⁶⁴ years: Black holes evaporate via Hawking radiation

10¹⁰⁰ years: Universe reaches maximum entropy

Alternative Scenarios

Big Rip

If dark energy continues to accelerate expansion, it could eventually overcome all fundamental forces, tearing apart galaxies, stars, atoms, and even space-time itself.

Big Crunch

If dark energy weakens or reverses, gravity could eventually halt expansion and cause the universe to collapse back into a singularity, reversing the Big Bang.

Cyclic Universe

Some theories propose the universe undergoes infinite cycles of expansion and contraction, with each Big Bang followed by a Big Crunch and subsequent rebirth.

Depends on dark energy Trillions of years Highly speculative
