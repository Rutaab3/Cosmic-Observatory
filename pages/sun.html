The Sun

Our nearest star, the source of all energy and life on Earth

Our Nearest Star

The Sun is the star at the heart of our solar system, a massive sphere of hot plasma heated to incandescence by nuclear fusion reactions in its core. Its gravity holds the entire solar system together, and its energy makes life on Earth possible.

As a G-type main sequence star, the Sun is about 4.6 billion years old and is expected to remain stable for another 5 billion years. It contains 99.8% of the solar system's mass and could fit about one million Earths inside it.

Diameter: 1.4 million km Mass: 1.989 × 10³⁰ kg Age: 4.6 billion years Temperature: 5,778 K surface

Solar Structure

The layers and regions of our star

Core

The Sun's core is where nuclear fusion occurs, converting hydrogen into helium and releasing enormous amounts of energy. Temperatures reach 15 million°C and pressures are 250 billion times Earth's atmospheric pressure.

Nuclear Fusion:

Every second, the Sun converts 600 million tons of hydrogen into helium, releasing energy equivalent to 100 billion hydrogen bombs.

Temperature: 15 million°C Density: 150 g/cm³ Radius: 20% of Sun

Radiative Zone

Energy from the core travels through the radiative zone as photons, taking thousands of years to reach the surface due to constant absorption and re-emission by particles.

Energy Transport:

Photons created in the core undergo a "random walk" through this dense region, bouncing between particles for 10,000 to 170,000 years before reaching the convective zone.

Temperature: 7 million°C to 2 million°C Extends to 70% of solar radius

Convective Zone

In the convective zone, hot plasma rises to the surface while cooler plasma sinks, creating convection currents that transport energy more efficiently than radiation.

Convection Cells:

These convection currents create granules visible on the Sun's surface, each about the size of Texas and lasting 8-20 minutes.

Temperature: 2 million°C to 5,778 K Outer 30% of Sun

Photosphere

The photosphere is the Sun's visible surface, the layer from which sunlight is emitted. It's about 500 km thick and has a temperature of approximately 5,778 K.

Sunspots:

Dark regions on the photosphere caused by intense magnetic fields that inhibit convection, making these areas cooler (about 3,800 K) and darker.

Temperature: 5,778 K Thickness: 500 km Visible light emission

Solar Atmosphere

The layers above the Sun's surface

Chromosphere

The chromosphere is a thin layer above the photosphere, visible as a reddish glow during solar eclipses. It's where the temperature begins to rise again, reaching 20,000 K at its top.

Spicules:

Jet-like features that shoot up from the chromosphere at speeds of 20 km/s, reaching heights of 10,000 km and lasting 5-10 minutes.

Solar Prominences:

Large, bright features extending outward from the Sun's surface, often in loop-like configurations following magnetic field lines.

Temperature: 4,000-20,000 K Thickness: 2,000 km Red hydrogen emission

Corona

The corona is the Sun's outermost atmosphere, extending millions of kilometers into space. Despite being farther from the core, it reaches temperatures of 1-3 million°C.

Coronal Heating Problem:

Scientists are still investigating why the corona is so much hotter than the photosphere, with magnetic reconnection being a leading theory.

Solar Wind:

The corona is the source of the solar wind, a stream of charged particles that flows throughout the solar system at speeds of 400-800 km/s.

Temperature: 1-3 million°C Extends millions of km Source of solar wind

Solar Activity

Dynamic phenomena on our active star

Solar Flares

Solar flares are sudden releases of electromagnetic energy from the Sun's surface, caused by magnetic reconnection in the corona. They can release energy equivalent to billions of hydrogen bombs.

Classification:

Solar flares are classified by their X-ray brightness: A, B, C, M, and X classes, with X-class being the most powerful.

Effects on Earth:

Large solar flares can disrupt radio communications, damage satellites, and cause power grid failures. They also enhance aurora displays at high latitudes.

Coronal Mass Ejections (CMEs):

Sometimes associated with flares, CMEs are massive bursts of solar wind and magnetic fields released from the corona, traveling at speeds up to 3,000 km/s.

Solar Cycle

The Sun follows an 11-year cycle of activity, from solar minimum (few sunspots) to solar maximum (many sunspots and flares).

Current Cycle

Solar Cycle 25 began in December 2019 and is expected to peak around 2025.

11-year cycle Magnetic field reversal Sunspot variation

Magnetic Field

The Sun's magnetic field is generated by the movement of charged particles in its interior. This field is responsible for most solar activity and follows an 11-year cycle.

Magnetic Reconnection:

When magnetic field lines break and reconnect, they release enormous amounts of energy, causing solar flares and heating the corona.

Sunspot Cycle:

Sunspots appear in pairs with opposite magnetic polarities and migrate from high latitudes toward the equator during each solar cycle.

Space Weather

Solar activity affects the space environment around Earth, creating "space weather" that can impact technology and human activities.

Geomagnetic Storms:

When CMEs interact with Earth's magnetosphere, they can cause geomagnetic storms that disrupt GPS, radio communications, and power grids.

Aurora:

Charged particles from the solar wind interact with Earth's atmosphere to create beautiful aurora displays near the magnetic poles.

Solar Evolution

The past, present, and future of our star

Life Cycle of the Sun

Formation (4.6 billion years ago)

The Sun formed from the gravitational collapse of a molecular cloud, with nuclear fusion beginning when core temperatures reached 10 million K.

Protostar phase T Tauri stage Main sequence begins
Current Phase (Present)

The Sun is currently in its main sequence phase, steadily converting hydrogen to helium in its core. It has been stable for 4.6 billion years.

Hydrogen burning Stable luminosity G-type star
Red Giant Phase (5 billion years future)

When hydrogen in the core is exhausted, the Sun will expand into a red giant, potentially engulfing Mercury and Venus.

Core contraction Outer layer expansion Helium burning
Planetary Nebula (7 billion years future)

The Sun will shed its outer layers, creating a beautiful planetary nebula with a white dwarf at the center.

Mass loss Nebula formation White dwarf remnant
White Dwarf (8+ billion years future)

The Sun will become a white dwarf, slowly cooling over trillions of years until it becomes a cold, dark object.

Earth-sized remnant Gradual cooling No nuclear fusion
