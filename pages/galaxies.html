Galaxies

Explore the vast cosmic islands containing billions of stars, gas, and dark matter

What are Galaxies?

Galaxies are massive collections of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter bound together by gravity. They are the fundamental building blocks of the universe, containing anywhere from millions to trillions of stars. Our own Milky Way galaxy is home to over 400 billion stars and spans approximately 100,000 light-years in diameter.

Scientists categorize galaxies based on their shapes, physical features, and the activity in their central regions. The classification system helps us understand how galaxies form, evolve, and interact with each other throughout cosmic time.

Types of Galaxies

Discover the different classifications and characteristics of cosmic islands

Spiral Galaxies

Our Milky Way is a prime example of spiral galaxies, characterized by their distinctive spiral arms that wind outward from a central bulge. These cosmic pinwheels contain a pancake-like disk of stars with arms that can be wound tightly or loosely.

Key Features:
Spiral Arms Central Bulge Stellar Disk Dark Matter Halo
Barred Spirals:

Two-thirds of spiral galaxies, including the Milky Way and Andromeda, are barred spirals. These galaxies feature ribbons of stars, gas, and dust that cut across their centers. The presence of a bar indicates that a galaxy has reached full maturity.

Milky Way: 100,000 ly diameter Andromeda: 220,000 ly diameter 400+ billion stars

Elliptical Galaxies

Elliptical galaxies have shapes ranging from completely round to oval. They are less common than spiral galaxies and typically contain little gas and dust, showing minimal organization or structure.

Characteristics:
Round to Oval Shape Little Gas & Dust Older Stars Random Orbits

The stars in elliptical galaxies orbit around the core in random directions and are generally older than those in spiral galaxies. Scientists believe elliptical galaxies originate from collisions and mergers between spiral galaxies.

Formation Theory:

These galaxies likely formed through galactic collisions and mergers, which disrupted the organized structure of spiral galaxies and redistributed their stellar populations into more random configurations.

Notable Elliptical Galaxies

M87 (Virgo A)
Distance: 53.5 million ly Mass: 6.6 trillion solar masses

Giant elliptical galaxy in the Virgo Cluster, famous for its supermassive black hole.

NGC 4472 (M49)
Distance: 55.9 million ly Diameter: 157,000 ly

Brightest galaxy in the Virgo Cluster with over 6,000 globular clusters.

IC 1101
Distance: 1.04 billion ly Diameter: 5.5 million ly

One of the largest known galaxies in the observable universe.

Lenticular Galaxies

Lenticular galaxies are a hybrid between spirals and ellipticals. They have the central bulge and disk common to spiral galaxies but lack the distinctive spiral arms.

Features:
Central Bulge Stellar Disk No Spiral Arms Older Stars
Evolution Theories:

Faded Spirals: These galaxies may be older spirals whose arms have faded over time due to the depletion of gas needed for star formation.

Merger Products: Alternatively, lenticulars may have formed from mergers of spiral galaxies that resulted in the loss of spiral structure.

Lenticular Examples

NGC 5866 (Spindle Galaxy)
Distance: 50 million ly Type: S0

Edge-on lenticular galaxy with a prominent dust lane.

NGC 2787
Distance: 24 million ly Diameter: 60,000 ly

Barred lenticular galaxy with a supermassive black hole.

Irregular Galaxies

Irregular galaxies have unusual shapes that don't fit into the standard spiral or elliptical categories. They can look like toothpicks, rings, or even small groupings of stars.

Size Range:
Dwarf: 100 million solar masses Large: 10 billion solar masses
Formation Causes:

Gravitational Interactions: Close encounters with larger galaxies can distort smaller galaxies, pulling away material and creating irregular shapes.

Galaxy Collisions: When galaxies collide, they can create larger, oddly shaped mashups that don't conform to standard classifications.

Tidal Forces: Strong gravitational forces from nearby massive galaxies can strip material and reshape smaller galaxies.

Famous Irregular Galaxies

Large Magellanic Cloud
Distance: 160,000 ly Diameter: 14,000 ly

Satellite galaxy of the Milky Way, visible from the Southern Hemisphere.

Small Magellanic Cloud
Distance: 200,000 ly Diameter: 7,000 ly

Companion to the Large Magellanic Cloud, also orbiting the Milky Way.

NGC 1427A
Distance: 62 million ly Speed: 600 km/s

Irregular galaxy being torn apart by the Fornax Cluster's gravity.

Active Galaxies

Galaxies powered by supermassive black holes

Active Galactic Nuclei

Around 10% of known galaxies are active, meaning their centers appear more than 100 times brighter than the combined light of their stars. This excess energy comes from areas near supermassive black holes at their centers.

Energy Source:

Gas and dust collect around the central black hole to form an accretion disk. The black hole's immense gravity compresses and heats this material, causing it to glow across multiple wavelengths from infrared to X-rays.

Structure:
Supermassive Black Hole Accretion Disk Dust Torus Relativistic Jets
Classification:

Radio-Loud: Emit strongly in radio wavelengths from both the accretion disk and jets.

Radio-Quiet: Have little to no emission from jets, primarily radiating from the accretion disk.

Active Galaxy Types

Seyfert Galaxies

Spiral galaxies with bright, variable nuclei. Type 1 shows broad emission lines, Type 2 shows narrow lines.

Quasars

Extremely luminous active galactic nuclei, often the most distant objects we can observe.

Blazars

Active galaxies with jets pointed directly toward Earth, appearing extremely bright and variable.

Radio Galaxies

Elliptical galaxies with powerful radio jets extending far beyond the visible galaxy.

Galaxy Formation & Evolution

How galaxies form and change over cosmic time

Formation Timeline

13.2 Billion Years Ago

First galaxies form from primordial gas clouds collapsing under gravity in the early universe.

10-12 Billion Years Ago

Peak era of galaxy formation and star formation. Many large galaxies establish their basic structure.

4.6 Billion Years Ago

Milky Way and Andromeda galaxies reach their current mature forms with well-defined spiral structure.

4.5 Billion Years Future

Predicted collision between Milky Way and Andromeda will create a new elliptical galaxy.

Evolutionary Processes

Galaxy Mergers:

When galaxies collide, they can merge to form larger galaxies. Major mergers often result in elliptical galaxies, while minor mergers can trigger star formation bursts.

Gas Accretion:

Galaxies grow by accreting gas from their surroundings. This fresh gas fuels continued star formation and galaxy growth.

Stellar Feedback:

Massive stars and supernovae inject energy into the interstellar medium, regulating star formation and driving galactic winds.

Environmental Effects:

Galaxy clusters can strip gas from member galaxies through ram pressure, affecting their evolution and star formation rates.

Dark Matter: 85% of galaxy mass Stars: 10% of galaxy mass Gas & Dust: 5% of galaxy mass
